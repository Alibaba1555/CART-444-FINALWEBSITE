PACMAN Variation Jam

This project begins with a simple question: what happens when a familiar system starts to break?

Based on the classic structure of Pac-Man, I developed a series of variations that gradually distort movement, perception, and spatial logic. Each mode is not just a gameplay change, but a small experiment on how rules shape experience.

Instead of designing a completely new game, I chose to work within an existing system — and push it until it feels unfamiliar.

View on GitHub ↗
Process

The project was developed through iterative experiments. I started from the original Pac-Man mechanics, then introduced controlled disruptions — limiting vision, cloning agents, deforming the maze, and altering pacing.

Each iteration tested a specific question: how much can the system change before it stops being recognizable?

Reflection

This project revealed how strongly players rely on learned patterns. Even small changes — like reduced visibility or slight spatial distortion — can create tension and uncertainty.

Looking back, the project could be pushed further by introducing more dynamic systems, such as adaptive AI or procedural level changes, allowing the game to respond to the player rather than remain fixed.

    Artist Statement

    Through photography, I seek to capture the traces of human presence — exploring identity and memory as they quietly unfold within space. Rather than focusing on the individual directly, my work is drawn to what remains: subtle marks, atmospheres, and moments that suggest a life once there.

    Warm light, overlooked corners, and fleeting encounters become ways of holding onto something otherwise passing. My images attempt to preserve not just what is seen, but the fragile evidence of being.

    On Beginning

    I started photography in December 2025. From the very first days, it felt less like learning a skill and more like finally having a way to look properly at the world. Whenever I had free time, I would go outside and record whatever caught my attention — a corner of light, a surface worn by use, a moment that felt worth keeping.

    These early photographs are not finished works. They are evidence of paying attention.
